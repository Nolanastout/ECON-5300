Data Exploration Assignment

Introduction

Walmart is interested in understanding whether temperature affects weekly sales at their stores. With average temperatures rising over time, understanding this relationship is important for business planning. I use panel data from 45 Walmart stores observed weekly from February 2010 through October 2012 to answer the research question: Do higher temperatures causally affect sales?

I find that temperature does affect sales, but the relationship is non-linear. Sales are maximized at moderate temperatures around 64 degrees fahrenheit. Both very cold weather and very hot weather are associated with lower sales compared to this optimal temperature. This inverted-U relationship is statistically significant and suggests that extreme temperatures deter shopping.

Data

The dataset contains weekly sales data from 45 Walmart stores spanning approximately 2.7 years. Each observation includes weekly sales (in dollars), average temperature (Fahrenheit), a holiday indicator, and economic variables including unemployment rate, fuel prices, and the Consumer Price Index (CPI).

{#Load libraries} library(tidyverse) library(lubridate) library(fixest)

{#Load the data} walmart <- read_csv("Walmart_Sales.csv")

{#Convert dates and create month variable} walmart <- walmart %>% mutate(Date = dmy(Date), Month = month(Date))

{#Look at summary statistics} summary(walmart)

The data contains 6,435 observations across 45 stores. Average weekly sales are about $1.05 million, with substantial variation (ranging from $210,000 to $3.8 million). Temperatures range from -2 degrees to 100 degrees, with a mean of 61 degrees.

Identification Strategy and Causal Diagram

To identify the causal effect of temperature on sales, I need to address confounding. My causal diagram shows the key challenges:

Main Back Door Paths:

  1. Temperature ← Month → Weekly Sales: Both temperature and sales vary by season. December has cold weather and high holiday sales. August has hot weather and back to school shopping. Without controlling for month, I would confuse seasonal shopping patterns with temperature effects.

  2. Temperature ← Store → Weekly Sales: Stores are located in different geographic areas with different climates. A store in Arizona has consistently high temperatures and may have different customer demographics than a store in Minnesota with consistently low temperatures. This creates a false correlation between temperature and sales.

To close these back doors, I use store fixed effects and month fixed effects. Store fixed effects control for all time-invariant characteristics of each store, including geographic location, store size, customer demographics, and management quality. Month fixed effects control for seasonal shopping patterns that repeat each year.

This identification strategy means I’m estimating the effect of temperature by comparing the same store to itself in the same month across different years. For example, I compare Store 1 in July 2010 (when it was 85 degrees) to Store 1 in July 2011 (when it was 75 degrees). This within-store, within-month variation isolates the temperature effect from confounders.

I also control for time-varying economic conditions (unemployment, fuel prices, CPI) and holiday weeks. Standard errors are clustered by store to account for correlation of observations within the same store over time.

## Regression Analysis

I estimate two models to test whether the temperature effect is linear or non-linear.

```{#Run a linear regression model using fixed effects for store and month}
m1 <- feols(Weekly_Sales ~ Temperature + Holiday_Flag + Unemployment + Fuel_Price + CPI | Store + Month, cluster = ~Store, data = walmart)

{#Look at results of the regreesion} summary(m1)

{#Check to see if the relationship between temperature and sales is non-linear using a quadratic} walmart <- walmart %>% mutate(Temperature_sq = Temperature^2)

{#Run model with temperature and temperature^2} m2 <- feols(Weekly_Sales ~ Temperature + Temperature_sq + Holiday_Flag + Unemployment + Fuel_Price + CPI | Store + Month, cluster = ~Store, data = walmart)

{#Look at results of the second regression} summary(m2)

{#Compare linear and quadratic models} etable(m1, m2)

Linear Model: In the linear specification, a 1°F increase in temperature is associated with a $696 increase in weekly sales (p = 0.034). This coefficient is statistically significant at the 5% level. However, this assumes the effect is constant at all temperature levels, which may not be realistic.

Quadratic Model: The quadratic model shows important non-linearity. The Temperature coefficient is 3,414 (p = 0.006) and the Temperature² coefficient is -26.67 (p = 0.021). Both coefficients are statistically significant. The negative coefficient on Temperature² indicates an inverted-U relationship - the marginal effect of temperature decreases as temperature increases.

Control Variables: - Holiday weeks have approximately $32,000-35,000 higher sales (highly significant) - Higher unemployment reduces sales by about $32,000 per percentage point increase (p < 0.01) - Higher fuel prices are associated with lower sales (marginally significant, p ≈ 0.06) - CPI is not statistically significant once other economic variables are controlled for

Model Fit: The quadratic model fits slightly better as the R² goes from 0.937 to 0.938, and the within-R² increases from 0.018 to 0.022. The high overall R² shows that store and month fixed effects explain most of the variation, while the within-R² shows that temperature and other time-varying factors explain about 2% of the remaining variation.ing the Optimal Temperature

For a quadratic function, the peak occurs where the slope equals zero. For my quadratic model this happens at temperature = -b1/(2*b2) which equals 64 degrees.

{#Calculate where sales peak by calculating: Maximum of Y = b1*X + b2*X^2 occurs at X = -b1/(2*b2)} b1 <- coef(m2)["Temperature"] b2 <- coef(m2)["Temperature_sq"] peak <- -b1 / (2 * b2) cat("Sales peak at", round(peak, 1), "degrees F\n")

Sales are maximized at 64 degrees. This means:

  • Below 64 degrees: Warmer weather increases sales. Moving from 50 degrees to 64 degrees increases sales.
  • At 64 degrees: Sales reach their maximum.
  • Above 64 degrees: Warmer weather decreases sales. Moving from 80 degrees to 64 degrees would increase sales.

This makes economic sense. Very cold weather may discourage shopping trips, while very hot weather may lead people to stay indoors in air conditioning or shift shopping to different times of day.

{#Plot the quadratic relationship} ggplot(walmart, aes(x = Temperature, y = Weekly_Sales)) + geom_point(alpha = 0.05) + stat_smooth(method = "lm", formula = y ~ poly(x, 2)) + labs(title = "Quadratic Relationship", x = "Temperature (F)", y = "Weekly Sales ($)")

The quadratic model visualizes the fitted quadratic relationship. The inverted-U curve clearly shows sales peaking at moderate temperatures and declining at both extremes.

Interpretation

{#Check model fit by comparing R-squared values to see how much variance each model explains} fitstat(m1, 'r2') fitstat(m2, 'r2')

Statistical Significance: The Temperature² coefficient has a p-value of 0.021, providing strong evidence of non-linearity. I reject the null hypothesis that the relationship is purely linear.

Economic Magnitude: The effects are economically meaningful. Based on the quadratic model: - Moving from 50 degrees to the optimal 64 degrees increases weekly sales by approximately $29,000 - Moving from 80 degrees to the optimal 64 degrees increases weekly sales by approximately $20,000 - A 10-degree deviation from optimal temperature reduces sales by roughly $10,000-15,000 per week

Why I Chose This Model: I tested the quadratic specification because it seemed plausible that extreme temperatures (both hot and cold) might deter shopping. The statistically significant Temperature² coefficient and improved model fit confirm this intuition was correct. The graph showing the relationship by month also suggested some curvature rather than a purely linear pattern.

Conclusion

This analysis provides strong evidence that temperature causally affects Walmart sales in a non-linear way. Using store and month fixed effects to control for confounding, I find that sales are maximized at moderate temperatures around 64 degrees, with both colder and hotter weather leading to reduced sales.

For Walmart Decision Makers:

  1. Temperature matters for sales: A 10 degree change from the optimal temperature can reduce weekly sales by $10,000-15,000 per store. This is a meaningful impact on revenue.

  2. Regional performance differences: Stores in consistently extreme climates (very hot Southwest or very cold Northern states) may underperform relative to stores in temperate regions. This should be considered when evaluating store performance and setting sales targets.

  3. Seasonal planning: Peak sales temperatures occur in spring and fall months. The temperature effect helps explain why sales patterns vary across seasons beyond just holiday effects.

Limitations: This analysis cannot separate temperature from other weather conditions like rain or humidity. The effect identified represents the total impact through all mechanisms - reduced foot traffic, changed shopping times, and altered purchasing behavior. Additionally, this analysis uses data from 2010-2012; consumer behavior may have changed with increased online shopping adoption.

Bottom Line: Temperature is not just a weather variable - it has a real, measurable impact on Walmart’s business. Stores experiencing unusual temperature patterns should not be penalized in performance evaluations, and temperature forecasts could inform promotional planning and staffing decisions.
